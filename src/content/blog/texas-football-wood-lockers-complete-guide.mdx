### Wood vs. Metal: The Texas Climate Factor

Texas presents one of the most challenging environments for locker room materials. Summer temperatures regularly exceed 100°F, humidity is high across much of the state—particularly in Houston, San Antonio, and East Texas—and the contrast between air-conditioned facilities and outdoor practice areas creates constant moisture cycling.

Metal lockers suffer in this environment. They dent easily, corrode over time when exposed to sweat and cleaning chemicals, and feel cold and industrial in ways that undermine the professional atmosphere programs are trying to create. Paint and finish on metal lockers deteriorates faster in high-humidity environments, making facilities look run-down within a few years of installation.

![Custom wood athletic lockers providing superior durability and professional appearance for Texas football programs](https://playerstall.b-cdn.net/images/IMG_8075.jpg)

Quality wood lockers handle the Texas environment significantly better when properly constructed. Solid plywood construction with sealed finishes resists moisture cycling, maintains dimensional stability, and holds up under daily use from large athletes. Wood is also naturally quieter—an important quality in locker rooms where coaches give pre-game and halftime talks.

The key is construction quality. High-density plywood with sealed joints, proper ventilation gaps in the locker design, and high-quality polyurethane or lacquer finishes protect the wood structure from moisture over the long term. From a financial standpoint, well-built wood lockers outlast metal alternatives by 5–10 years in Texas programs. When you factor in the total cost of ownership—replacement costs, maintenance, and the recruiting and culture value of a professional appearance—wood lockers are the clear long-term investment for programs serious about sustained excellence.

### Sizing for Texas Football Equipment

Football equipment demands more locker space than any other team sport. A complete Texas football player's gear includes:

- Game helmet and practice helmet
- Game shoulder pads and practice shoulder pads
- Home jersey, away jersey, and practice jersey (multiple pairs)
- Game pants and practice pants
- Cleats (often multiple pairs)
- Gloves, undershirts, socks, and accessories
- Personal items and electronics

This volume of equipment means football lockers need to be sized correctly from the start. Undersized lockers create immediate problems—gear gets crammed in, equipment is damaged, and the locker room looks disorganized no matter how much effort goes into cleaning.

**Recommended sizing for Texas football programs:**

- **Width:** 30"–36" per player. Wider is better for offensive and defensive linemen who carry more equipment bulk. Programs with undersized budgets sometimes go to 24" and regret it immediately.
- **Height:** 72"–84". Standard 72" works for most programs. Programs with tall rosters may want 78"–84" to allow better jersey hanging and overhead storage.
- **Depth:** 24". This is standard and works well for football equipment.
- **Shelf configuration:** A fixed upper shelf for helmets, a main rod for jerseys, a lower shelf for shoulder pads and cleats, and a personal storage cubby are the recommended baseline.

Texas programs vary in squad size from roughly 40 players at smaller 3A schools to 100+ at large 6A programs and college teams. Always plan for your largest realistic roster, not your current roster—adding lockers to a completed installation is more expensive than building the right number from the start.

### Ventilation and Odor Control in Texas Heat

Football equipment is one of the most challenging odor-management problems in all of sports. Shoulder pads, helmets, and practice gear absorb sweat through every practice and game, and Texas summer temperatures accelerate bacterial growth that creates persistent odors.

Proper ventilation in locker design is the first line of defense. PlayerStall lockers include ventilation gaps in locker doors and side panels that allow air circulation around equipment—a critical feature that metal locker designs often neglect in favor of solid-panel aesthetics. This airflow prevents the moisture buildup that causes both odor and equipment degradation.

**Best practices for Texas football programs:**

- Specify ventilated locker doors as standard in your design
- Ensure your HVAC system provides adequate air exchange in the locker room (consult your facilities manager)
- Establish a post-practice equipment airing routine—helmets and shoulder pads should be removed from lockers and aired for at least 30 minutes after practice
- Use antimicrobial sprays on pads and helmet pads weekly during the season
- Install exhaust fans specifically in the locker room space if the HVAC system is undersized

Wood's natural breathability also helps compared to sealed metal. The slightly porous nature of finished wood allows the locker interior to breathe in ways that all-metal construction cannot match.
