## How to Get Budget Approval for a Locker Room Upgrade: A Guide for Athletic Directors

**Getting budget approval for a locker room upgrade is one of the most consistently frustrating challenges athletic directors face. The need is clear. The benefits are obvious to anyone who has watched a recruit walk through an outdated facility. But translating that clarity into a successful budget request requires a specific approach—one that speaks the language of athletic program value in terms that administration actually approves.**

**This guide is about the approval process, not the locker room design. It covers how to frame the investment, what data to use, how to structure a proposal that moves through review, and how to handle the objections you'll inevitably encounter. If you're a coach or athletic department staff member making the case to an AD, this guide works in reverse—it tells you what the AD needs to take upstairs.**

### Key Takeaways

- Budget requests framed as maintenance costs almost never succeed; requests framed as recruiting and program investments succeed at much higher rates
- The strongest approval cases combine three elements: concrete recruiting impact data, comparison to peer or competitor facilities, and a total cost of ownership analysis that makes the upfront investment look different
- Administration often needs to see the request in specific terms that connect to institutional priorities—enrollment, retention, institutional reputation, donor engagement
- Naming opportunities and development potential can convert a net cost into a partial or fully funded project—this reframes the entire conversation
- The timing of your request matters: budget cycles, capital projects calendars, and administrative priorities should inform when you make your case

### Why Most Budget Requests Fail

Most locker room budget requests fail for the same reason: they make the wrong case to the wrong audience.

The typical request goes something like this: "Our current lockers are 20 years old and in poor condition. We need to replace them. The cost is $X." This frames the request as a maintenance expense—a cost with no return, competing against every other deferred maintenance item in the facilities queue.

Administration hears this request in a specific context. They're looking at a capital projects backlog, a deferred maintenance list, restricted budget allocations, and competing requests from every department in the institution. A maintenance framing puts your locker room upgrade in direct competition with HVAC repairs, roof replacements, and technology infrastructure. In that competition, lockers rarely win.

The requests that succeed don't compete in the maintenance bucket. They compete in the investment bucket—alongside recruiting initiatives, enrollment strategies, and institutional reputation projects. Getting your request into that bucket requires a different framing, different data, and a different audience.

### The Investment Framing That Works

The most successful locker room budget cases are built on a single foundational argument: *this is a recruiting investment, and here is its return*.

College athletics is a talent acquisition business. Recruiting is the core competitive function of every program. Every dollar of recruiting investment—scholarships, coaching salaries, campus visit programs—is justified by its contribution to talent acquisition. A locker room upgrade is a facility investment that directly affects recruiting outcomes. Frame it that way.

The specific elements of the investment framing:

**Recruiting conversion impact.** Every official campus visit includes a facility tour. A strong facility converts more visits to commitments than a weak one. If you can reasonably estimate that an upgraded locker room improves your visit-to-commitment conversion rate by even five percentage points, what is that worth in recruiting value? Calculate it. One additional scholarship athlete who commits to your program because of a facility they couldn't stop talking about—one who might have gone elsewhere—represents hundreds of thousands of dollars in scholarship value and competitive value over their career.

**Cost per recruit impression.** A locker room that costs $50,000 and influences 20 official visitors per year creates 400 meaningful facility impressions over a 20-year lifespan. That's $125 per impressionable recruit moment. Compare that to the cost-per-impression of any other recruiting channel in your budget.

**Retention and culture.** The locker room isn't just a recruiting tool—it's a daily environment for the players already in your program. Athletes who feel valued and well-supported are more likely to stay enrolled, maintain eligibility, and perform at higher levels. The locker room is a visible, daily expression of how much the institution values its athletes.

### Building Your Recruiting Impact Case

Administration responds to evidence. The stronger your data, the stronger your case. Here's how to build it:

**Survey your coaching staff.** Ask your coaches directly: "How many times in the past two years has a recruit or their family specifically mentioned our facility as a concern or limitation?" Coaches know these conversations happen. Getting them on record—in writing if possible—creates evidence that the current facility is costing you in recruiting.

**Gather peer program data.** Research what your direct competitors and peer institutions have invested in similar facilities. Conference rivals who have recently upgraded are your best comparison cases. If the program two slots above you in your conference just completed a flagship locker room and is using it prominently in their recruiting materials, that's direct competitive pressure that administration can understand.

**Review recruit destination data.** For the recruits you've lost in recent years who chose a competing program—do you know what their decision factors were? If any cited facilities (directly or in the campus visit debrief), document it. Exit interview data from transfers can also be relevant.

**Pull published research.** Studies on athlete facility satisfaction and recruiting outcomes exist in the sports management literature. <a href="https://www.ncaa.org/research" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">NCAA research publications</a> occasionally address facility investment and competitive performance correlations. While you won't find a study that says precisely "locker room upgrades increase win rate by X%," you can find evidence that facility quality is a documented factor in recruit decision-making.

**Use the cost-of-a-missed-recruit calculation.** Work backwards from the value of a scholarship athlete. For a Division I scholarship, you're talking about $25,000–$65,000 in institutional aid per year, times four years, plus the competitive value of having that athlete on your roster rather than a competitor's. One missed recruit is a meaningful number. Make that calculation explicit.

### The Total Cost of Ownership Argument

Budget conversations often get stuck on sticker price. The total cost of ownership (TCO) analysis reframes the price question in a way that makes higher-quality investment look different.

The TCO comparison for locker rooms:

**Scenario A: Premium custom wood lockers**
- Upfront cost: $50,000 (example, college program)
- Maintenance cost: Low (sealed wood surfaces, minimal wear parts)
- Expected service life: 15–20 years
- Replacement cycle: One purchase over 20 years
- TCO over 20 years: ~$55,000–$60,000 (including occasional maintenance)

**Scenario B: Commodity metal lockers**
- Upfront cost: $25,000
- Maintenance cost: Moderate (corrosion, dents, hinge failures, repainting)
- Expected service life: 8–12 years in active athletic use
- Replacement cycle: Two purchases over 20 years
- TCO over 20 years: ~$55,000–$65,000 (two purchases plus maintenance)

These numbers will vary by program size and market, but the pattern is consistent: the premium upfront cost of quality wood lockers is largely or fully offset by longevity over a 20-year program lifecycle. Present this analysis with your actual quotes, and the "it's too expensive" objection loses most of its force.

Add to this the recruiting value differential over the same period—years of facilities that support recruiting vs. years of facilities that undermine it—and the TCO case becomes compelling.

### Peer and Competitor Facility Comparison

Nothing moves administration faster than peer comparison. Institutions are acutely sensitive to falling behind peer programs, and athletic facilities are visible, documentable, and directly comparable.

Build a facilities comparison analysis that includes:

**Direct competitors in your recruiting market.** What do their locker rooms look like? Do they post facility photos on recruiting pages, social media, or recruiting apps? Can you show specific examples of competitor facilities that outperform yours?

**Conference peers.** What has your conference done in facility investment over the past five years? If three of your conference peers have upgraded locker rooms in the past cycle and you haven't, you can document a specific competitive gap.

**National standards for your division level.** What does a standard-issue facility look like at the D-I, D-II, or NAIA level in your sport? If your facility is below divisional standard, document that explicitly.

Frame this data not as "we're behind"—which sounds like a complaint—but as "here is the specific competitive disadvantage we're addressing and how this investment closes it."

### Naming Opportunities and Development Strategy

This is the piece many athletic directors underuse: a well-presented locker room project is also a donor engagement opportunity that can offset or fully fund the investment cost.

Locker room naming opportunities are among the most accessible major gift vehicles in athletic fundraising. Individual locker nameplates are affordable ($500–$5,000 for a personalized nameplate sponsorship), while the room itself or named sections can command five- to six-figure gifts from the right donor base.

Consider structuring your budget proposal with a development component:

- Total project cost: $50,000
- Anticipated naming/sponsorship revenue: $20,000–$30,000
- Net institutional budget request: $20,000–$30,000

This structure halves the budget conversation. It also positions the project as a development opportunity—which changes the audience for the approval. Development staff become allies rather than bystanders.

Identify your top three to five prospects for locker room giving before the budget conversation. Know what naming opportunities you're offering and at what levels. Walk into the budget meeting with a development plan, not just a facilities request.

### Structuring the Proposal Document

A formal proposal document—even a well-organized one-page summary—dramatically increases approval rates compared to verbal requests or email conversations. Here's a structure that works:

**Executive summary (3–4 sentences).** State the request, the investment level, and the primary return. "We are requesting $X for a locker room upgrade that will strengthen our recruiting position, provide a [X]-year service life, and create naming opportunities worth an estimated $X in development revenue."

**Current state assessment.** Brief, factual description of the current locker room condition. Age, visible wear, functional limitations. Photos help. Don't editorialize—describe.

**Proposed investment.** Specific scope, vendor, design specifications, and timeline. Show that you've done the work.

**Recruiting and program impact.** The case you've built above—recruiting data, coach testimony, competitive comparison.

**Total cost of ownership analysis.** The 20-year comparison. Show your work.

**Development opportunity.** Naming options and revenue estimates.

**Timeline.** When installation would happen, when the facility would be ready.

**Recommendation.** Clear ask with the approved budget figure.

Keep it concise. Administrators who approve capital requests see many of them. A tight, well-organized proposal signals preparation and seriousness.

### Handling Common Objections

**"We have more pressing maintenance needs."** Acknowledge competing priorities directly, then distinguish: "This isn't a maintenance request—it's a strategic investment in our recruiting position. I've outlined how the cost compares favorably to two replacement cycles of the alternative, and we have a development plan that offsets a significant portion of the cost."

**"Can we do this cheaper?"** "We looked at lower-cost alternatives and they carry a shorter service life and fewer customization options. Over 20 years, the lower-cost option likely costs more in total, and it doesn't create the same recruiting asset. I'd rather make this case once for the right investment than come back in ten years for a replacement."

**"What's the ROI?"** Use the cost-per-recruit-impression calculation and the missed-recruit calculation. "The direct ROI is difficult to isolate, but here's how I'm thinking about it: [use your specific numbers]."

**"We don't have capital for this in the current budget."** Ask about alternative funding vehicles: "I'd like to explore whether development revenue and a naming campaign could fund a portion of this project. Could you connect me with the development office to assess the opportunity before we table the conversation?"

### Frequently Asked Questions

#### What's the single most persuasive argument for a locker room upgrade?

The missed-recruit calculation, made specific with actual numbers from your program. How many recruits have you lost to a comparable program with a better facility in the past three years? What is the scholarship and competitive value of those athletes? Making that number explicit and putting it next to the locker room cost changes the conversation.

#### Should I involve coaches in the budget request?

Yes. Coach testimony—especially written documentation of specific recruiting conversations where facility was cited as a concern—is among the most persuasive evidence you can bring. Coaches are also more likely to become vocal advocates for the project if they're involved in building the case from the beginning.

#### How do I find data on what competitor programs have spent on facilities?

Public institutions often disclose capital expenditures in their budget documents. Athletics-specific facility investments are sometimes covered in local media at the time of the project. Conference peer data can sometimes be gathered through athletic director peer networks. And the facilities themselves are often visible on official athletic websites, social media, and recruiting platforms.

#### What's the best timing in the budget cycle for a locker room request?

Most institutions have annual capital budget cycles with specific submission windows. Know your institution's cycle. Requests submitted early in the cycle window—not in the last week—receive more thoughtful consideration. Plan to have your proposal ready at least two months before the budget submission deadline.

#### How long should the locker room proposal take to get approved?

Budget approval timelines vary significantly by institution. Simple requests at institutions with straightforward capital approval processes may move in 30–60 days. Larger requests at institutions with more formal capital review processes may take a full budget cycle—three to six months. Plan accordingly.

### Conclusion

Getting budget approval for a locker room upgrade isn't primarily about making a case that the current lockers are worn out. It's about making a case that a new locker room is a competitive asset with a measurable return on investment—one that speaks the language of recruiting performance, total cost of ownership, and development opportunity.

Athletic directors who succeed in these budget conversations do the work upfront: they gather recruiting data, build the peer comparison, structure the TCO analysis, and identify the development opportunity before they walk into the room. They speak to institutional priorities, not just facility needs.

The locker room you're trying to build is worth the effort of this process. The programs that have great locker rooms built them through exactly this kind of systematic, evidence-based internal advocacy.
